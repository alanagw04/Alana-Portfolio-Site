Learn the Basics

Simple, direct answers to common cybersecurity questions.

1. What makes a password strong?

Historically, people were told to create passwords with weird symbols, numbers, and capital letters (e.g. Pa$$w0rd!). Computer scripts guess these very quickly. Today, security experts advocate for length over complexity.

A Passphrase uses 4 or more random, dictionary words combined. They are easy for humans to picture and remember, but take computers trillions of years to guess.

Examples Compared

Weak & Hard to Remember

P@ssw0rd!12

Short, common letters, easy for hackers to crack.
Strong & Easy to Remember

crater window violin yellow

Long, simple words. Write this down in a secure drawer.

2. Two-Factor Authentication (2FA)

Think of Two-Factor Authentication as a second lock on your front door. Even if a thief manages to copy or steal your primary door key (your password), they still cannot enter without the second key (a code sent to your phone or app).

This ensures that even if your password is leaked in a corporate database breach, your personal account remains locked. We recommend enabling 2FA on your critical accounts (email, bank, and medical portals).

Key Benefit: If you receive an unexpected code on your phone, it means someone else guessed your password. Do not enter the code, and change your password immediately.

3. CAPTCHAs (Security Verification Grids)

A CAPTCHA is a test to verify that you are a real human, not a spam program. Websites do this by showing you wavy text, or a grid of pictures, and asking you to click certain items (like traffic lights or crosswalks).

These represent a significant barrier for screen reader users, those with physical tremors, or those with visual difficulty. Most CAPTCHAs offer an Audio option (look for a small headphones icon) that speaks numbers out loud instead of showing pictures.

Avoid Online Scams

Learn to spot malicious messages and test messages you've received.

5 Common Scam Tactics

1. Tech Support Alerts

What it looks like: A pop-up appears warning that your computer is infected, showing a phone number to call immediately.

What to do instead: Close the browser. Real tech support companies (like Microsoft or Apple) never display phone numbers in warning screens.

2. Grandchild in Trouble

What it looks like: A caller claiming to be a grandchild, lawyer, or police officer demands money for emergency bail, requesting secrecy.

What to do instead: Hang up. Call your grandchild or their parents directly on their known phone number to verify the story.

3. Fake Bank/Government Alerts

What it looks like: A text warning that your bank account is frozen or that the IRS demands immediate payment for back taxes.

What to do instead: Never reply. Open your web browser, look up the official phone number of your bank or the agency, and call them directly.

4. Romance Scams

What it looks like: A friend met online quickly declares love, then requests funds for flights, medical issues, or bad investments.

What to do instead: Cut off contact. Never send money, gift cards, or wire transfers to individuals you have not met in person.

5. Prize or Lottery Scams

What it looks like: A letter or email claims you won a lottery, but requires paying a processing fee or tax before release.

What to do instead: Discard the notice. If you did not buy a ticket, you did not win a contest.

Helpful Verification Tips:

  • Audio alternative: Look for a headphones icon in real grids to hear the verification phrase instead of looking at images.
  • No time limits: Most CAPTCHAs are not timed. Take your time to examine each image tile.
  • Zoom helper: Press Ctrl and + on Windows to magnify the grid elements for easier viewing.
  • Skip or refresh: If a grid is too blurry or difficult to verify, click the refresh button to get a new set of images without penalties.

Account Recovery Manual

Direct, step-by-step checklists to resolve common login roadblocks.

I'm Locked Out

01.

Locate the Forgot Password link directly on the login form. Do not call numbers found on Google search.

02.

Enter your username. Check your email or text messages on your registered phone for a verification link.

03.

Input the code. When prompted, create a new Passphrase. Write it down immediately on physical paper.

04.

Log in using the new details and verify that your backup recovery email is set correctly.

Lost / Replaced My Device

01.

Transfer your phone number (SIM card) to your new mobile device to ensure text message (SMS) codes arrive.

02.

Locate the Recovery Codes sheet you generated when first setting up your authenticator app.

03.

If you lack codes, click "Try another way" on the login screen to receive verification via email or backup contact.

04.

Re-download the authenticator app and register the website's credentials to start generating codes again.

I Think I was Compromised

01.

Disconnect: Turn off your computer's internet or Wi-Fi immediately to prevent remote hackers from grabbing data.

02.

Use a separate, safe device (like your phone) to log in and change your email and primary banking passwords immediately.

03.

Log into your bank accounts, look for unauthorized transactions, and call their customer fraud line to freeze assets.

04.

Contact your family or a trusted technician to review your device's security status before connecting online again.
